# CEO Rules — Website Content Foundation

**Document type:** Content strategy & messaging foundation
**Phase:** Foundation only — *no design system, no final copy, no visuals*
**Version:** 1.0
**Prepared by:** OnShore Labs

> This document decides **what** the website says, **how** it says it, and **why** each piece
> exists — before a single color, font, or animation is chosen. If this foundation is wrong,
> every beautiful thing built on top of it will be wrong too. Read it fully before the next phase.

## 1. The North Star

Every content decision answers exactly one question:

> ### "Does this feel like CEO Rules?"

Not *is it premium, modern, clever, or impressive.* Only *does it feel like them.*

And every line of copy exists to reinforce one sentence — the core of the brand:

> ### CEO Rules is a house that teaches self-made men the quiet language of confidence through timeless craftsmanship.

If a piece of content doesn't move that sentence forward, cut it.

---

## 2. Positioning & Emotional Territory

**What we are actually selling is not clothing.** It's *confidence that feels earned.* The
customer walks in believing he needs better clothes and walks out believing he has become a
better version of himself. That transformation is the product.

**The territory we own:** the transition from **success to significance.** Not wealth. Not
fashion. Not status. Our customer is often *already* successful — and success brought a quieter,
unspoken insecurity:

> *Do I belong in these rooms? Am I dressed correctly? Am I making mistakes without realising it?
> Am I trying too hard?*

He never says these things aloud. The website's job is to **answer them without being asked** —
and therefore to **reduce anxiety, not inflate aspiration.** The visitor should leave feeling
*"I've found someone who understands,"* never *"I've found another luxury brand."*

The before → after we design toward:

| Before CEO Rules | After CEO Rules |
|---|---|
| "I have money." | "I have presence." |
| "I hope people take me seriously." | "I no longer need to ask." |
| "I'm wearing expensive clothes." | "I'm wearing clothes that belong to me." |

### The productive contradictions — preserve, never flatten

The name says one thing; the reality says the opposite. That tension is the brand's single
biggest differentiator. **Do not resolve it. Celebrate it.** Use this as a checklist — good
content holds *both* sides at once:

- The name sounds like **hustle / ambition** → the reality is **heritage, patience, quiet.**
- **Power** delivered with **warmth.**
- **Luxury** that stays **approachable.**
- **Education** offered inside **exclusivity.**
- **Mumbai** personality meeting **European tailoring** — without pretending to be European.
- **145-year tradition** carried by a **contemporary** voice.

If a section feels like it could belong to *any* premium tailor in the world, it has flattened a
contradiction and must be rewritten.

## 3. The Founder Voice — Anis-Led

**Anis Soomar is the medium through which the brand exists** — the mentor, the voice, the face, the
emotional center. Without him, CEO Rules is another tailoring business; with him, it's a philosophy.
Everything visible on Instagram says the same thing: **Anis does not enjoy selling — he enjoys
teaching.** That is the whole difference, and the site is built on it.

**The framing:**
- **Anis is the singular voice.** Warm, unhurried, mentor-first. The copy is *him* talking — not a
  faceless "house," not a committee.
- **Arshad is acknowledged once** — as the brother who keeps the house alongside him. Real, honest,
  present in the lineage, but never sharing the spotlight or splitting the voice.
- The **Haji Soomar lineage since 1881** frames the house — passed down, not a startup — with Anis
  as its living soul.
- Anis appears **naturally throughout** the experience, not quarantined in one "About" section.

**Who the voice is** — always these, never the words on the right:

| The voice is… | Never… |
|---|---|
| Mentor, teacher | "Founder" |
| Craftsman, guide | "CEO" |
| Storyteller, advisor | "Owner / businessman" |

**The copy-generating question** — hold this before writing *every* section:

> *"What would Anis naturally say if you walked into the store?"*

That answer becomes the copy. Not marketing language. What a wise tailor would actually say to a
man he respects.

## 4. Tone & Voice Guide

*This is the heart of the foundation. Everything downstream inherits from here.*

### Voice principles
1. **Guide, never sell.** The visitor is advised, not converted.
2. **Teach as generosity, not marketing.** Give knowledge away freely. Trust is the return.
3. **Confident and quiet.** Luxury whispers; it never shouts. No exclamation marks. No superlatives.
4. **Warm and human, and Mumbai-grounded.** Do *not* fake a European accent. The warmth is local.
5. **Restrained.** Few words. One idea per line. Let silence and space carry weight.
6. **Every section leaves the visitor slightly wiser** (the education rule, Section 4.4).

### Do / Don't — with concrete rewrites

| Instinct (✗) | CEO Rules (✓) |
|---|---|
| "Book now — premium bespoke suits at unbeatable prices!" | "When you're ready, we're on Ambedkar Road." |
| "We are Mumbai's #1 luxury tailor since 1881!" | "A cut of cloth. A hundred and forty-five years of knowing what to do with it." |
| "Our expert master tailors craft world-class garments." | "A jacket is built, not sewn. We'll show you the difference." |
| "Shop our exclusive collection of designer suits." | "This isn't a catalogue. It's what confidence tends to look like." |
| "About the Founder / Meet our CEO." | "Meet the man who'll teach you the difference." |

### Lexicon

**Words we use:** house · atelier · craft · cloth · cut · construction · presence · belong ·
learn · measure · time · hands · fit · carry (as in *carry yourself*).

**Words we ban:** buy now · shop · sale · offer · deal · #1 / best / world-class · hustle · grind ·
"luxury" used as filler decoration · exclamation marks · urgency ("limited time", "don't miss").

### Rhythm & reading
- Editorial, not corporate. Sentence fragments are allowed and encouraged.
- One idea per line. Short paragraphs. Generous silence between thoughts.
- Copy **supports** the image; it never fills space. If a line isn't earning its place, delete it.

### 4.4 The education rule (woven, not a section)
Because CEO Rules educates as generosity, **each homepage section must hand the visitor one small,
genuine takeaway** — a fit truth, a fabric fact, or a point of etiquette. Examples of the *kind*
of knowledge (not final copy):
- Why a jacket's shoulder is the one thing tailoring can't fake.
- What "canvassed" construction means and why it outlives you.
- The difference between expensive and *appropriate.*

The visitor should leave having learned something **even if they never become a customer.** That
is the trust engine.

## 5. Heritage as a Plot Twist

Heritage is important — but **heritage is not the headline.** Most luxury brands open with
"Established 1881 · Family Owned · Legacy." Customers don't connect with dates; they connect with
meaning.

**The rule:** the visitor becomes *emotionally invested first.* Only *then* do they discover the
age of the house — as a quiet reveal, almost a plot twist. At that moment, *"this house has
existed since 1881"* suddenly means something. **145 years earns its weight by arriving late,
not first.**

This is a hard sequencing constraint on the sitemap (Section 6): the number **1881** does not
appear in the Hero or Manifesto. It lands in the **Heritage** section, after Curiosity → Respect
→ the beginnings of Trust.

## 6. Sitemap & Emotional Arc

**One cinematic homepage**, experienced as a single continuous story — never "a collection of
sections." Each section inherits the emotion of the one before and hands a new one forward. The
momentum is **never reset.**

```
Curiosity → Respect → Trust → Connection → Aspiration → Action
```

| # | Section | Produces | Hands forward |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hero | Curiosity | "this isn't a tailoring site" |
| 2 | Brand Manifesto | Connection (belief) | what the house believes |
| 3 | Identity | Recognition | "this is about me, not clothes" |
| 4 | Heritage *(the 1881 reveal)* | Respect | earned depth |
| 5 | The Craft | Trust (mastery) | obsession you can see |
| 6 | Meet Anis Soomar | Connection (human) | "I'd listen to this man" |
| 7 | The Wardrobe | Aspiration | "I want to become this" |
| 8 | Testimonials | Reassurance | "others like me belong here" |
| 9 | Visit CEO Rules | Action | the natural next step |
| — | Footer | Calm close | — |

*No extra sections.* If a section can be removed without weakening the arc, it should not exist.

## 7. Per-Section Content Spec

For each section: **Purpose · Emotion · Content it holds · Must say · Must never do · Education
takeaway · Assets.** This is the blueprint the copywriting + build phase executes against.
*(Copy shown is direction, not final wording.)*

### Section 1 — Hero
- **Purpose:** Immediate curiosity. The visitor instantly senses this is *not* another tailoring site.
- **Emotion:** Curiosity.
- **Content:** One large cinematic image or slow video. One restrained line of copy. Wordmark. One primary CTA.
- **Must say:** A line that intrigues without explaining — presence over product. (Dir: *"The quiet language of confidence."*)
- **Must never do:** No "bespoke suits since 1881." No hero shot of a man smiling in a suit. No clutter. No date.
- **Education takeaway:** None here — curiosity only. The teaching begins in §2.
- **CTA:** *Visit the Atelier* (primary) / *Book a Consultation.*
- **Assets:** Cinematic hero (real — **to shoot**). IG screenshot = temporary placeholder only.

### Section 2 — Brand Manifesto
- **Purpose:** Introduce the *philosophy*, not the business.
- **Emotion:** Connection — the visitor starts to understand what the house believes.
- **Content:** A short manifesto — the belief system behind CEO Rules, in the house voice.
- **Must say:** That clothes are the medium, not the message; that confidence is taught, not bought.
- **Must never do:** No services list. No "we offer." No feature bullets.
- **Education takeaway:** A first idea — e.g. the difference between *expensive* and *appropriate.*
- **Assets:** Texture / atmosphere imagery (**to shoot**); mostly typographic.

### Section 3 — Identity
- **Purpose:** Say what CEO Rules actually represents — confidence & transformation, not clothing.
- **Emotion:** Recognition ("this is about me").
- **Content:** The before → after transformation (Section 2 table) expressed as narrative, not a grid of products.
- **Must say:** That the real product is *presence* — success carried gracefully.
- **Must never do:** Do not show or list garments here. No product framing.
- **Education takeaway:** What "carrying yourself" actually means — a note on posture/fit/self.
- **Assets:** Portrait-of-presence imagery (**to shoot**).

### Section 4 — Heritage *(the 1881 reveal)*
- **Purpose:** Reveal the heritage *naturally* — a discovery, not an "About" section.
- **Emotion:** Respect.
- **Content:** The Haji Soomar lineage (Crawford Market, 1881), carried to Anis (with brother Arshad) today. The number **1881 lands here** for the first time.
- **Must say:** 145 years; family custody; continuity as trust — the line that leads to Anis.
- **Must never do:** Do not lead with the date. No dry timeline. No "our story began…"
- **Education takeaway:** Why time is the one ingredient in tailoring that cannot be shortcut.
- **Assets:** Archival / store / heritage imagery (**to shoot / to source from family**).

### Section 5 — The Craft
- **Purpose:** Demonstrate mastery — through visuals, not paragraphs.
- **Emotion:** Trust (this is obsession, not manufacturing).
- **Content:** Fabric, measurement, construction, detail, precision — shown, barely narrated.
- **Must say:** Very little. Let a few captions teach; let the images carry.
- **Must never do:** No walls of text. No spec-sheet bragging.
- **Education takeaway:** One real construction truth (e.g. canvassed vs fused, the shoulder).
- **Assets:** Close, textured craft photography — hands, chalk, cloth, needle (**to shoot**; IG reels = placeholders).

### Section 6 — Meet Anis Soomar
- **Purpose:** Humanise the brand. **This is the emotional center of the site.**
- **Emotion:** Connection — "I'd actually listen to this man."
- **Content:** **Anis** as mentor and custodian — warm, personal, unhurried; a teacher, not a seller. His brother **Arshad** gets one honest line as co-keeper of the house.
- **Must say:** Who Anis is, and why he teaches rather than sells.
- **Must never do:** Never "Founder / CEO." No corporate headshots. No résumé. Don't split focus onto both brothers equally — this is Anis's section.
- **Education takeaway:** A piece of advice Anis would actually give a younger man.
- **Assets:** Real, warm portraits of Anis + ideally short founder video (**to shoot + interview** — hard dependency).

### Section 7 — The Wardrobe
- **Purpose:** Show the *outcome* as **identity**, not products.
- **Emotion:** Aspiration ("I want to become this version of myself").
- **Content:** Bespoke suits, bandhgalas, sherwanis, accessories — presented **editorially**.
- **Must say:** These are the shapes confidence takes — framed as identity, not SKUs.
- **Must never do:** **Never an e-commerce catalogue.** No prices, no "add to cart," no grid-of-thumbnails.
- **Education takeaway:** When a bandhgala outranks a suit — a note on occasion & appropriateness.
- **Assets:** Editorial fashion photography (**to shoot**).

### Section 8 — Testimonials
- **Purpose:** Reinforce trust.
- **Emotion:** Reassurance — "people like me belong here."
- **Content:** A few genuine, simple, elegant customer voices.
- **Must say:** Real words from real clients — transformation, not "great service."
- **Must never do:** No star ratings, no corporate logos, no wall of quotes. Not corporate.
- **Education takeaway:** Optional — a client's own realisation counts as teaching.
- **Assets:** Real testimonials (**to collect** — dependency). Do not fabricate.

### Section 9 — Visit CEO Rules
- **Purpose:** Remove friction. By now the visitor already *wants* to visit; this just makes it easy.
- **Emotion:** Action.
- **Content:** **Both** Bandra addresses (Ambedkar Road & Khar Linking Road), map, WhatsApp, opening hours, consultation CTA.
- **Must say:** Where to find the house and how to begin — as an invitation, not a pitch.
- **Must never do:** No hard sell. No urgency. No form that feels like a lead-capture funnel.
- **Education takeaway:** What to bring / expect at a first consultation (reduces the visitor's anxiety).
- **Assets:** Store photography + accurate map/contact data (**to confirm with client**).

### Footer
- **Purpose:** A calm close.
- **Content:** Minimal — wordmark, essential links, contact, social. Nothing unnecessary.
- **Must never do:** No newsletter nag, no link farm, no "subscribe for 10% off."

## 8. CTA & Conversion Philosophy

- The website is **never sales-driven.** By the end of the arc, visiting the store already feels
  like the inevitable next step — CTAs merely *remove friction.*
- **Primary action:** *Book a Consultation* / *Visit the Atelier.*
- **Secondary action:** *WhatsApp* (Mumbai-natural, low-pressure).
- **Placement rhythm:** a CTA appears when it's *earned* — quietly in the Hero, then not again
  until the visitor has been given a reason (post-Craft / post-Founder), and finally in Visit.
  Present, never pushy. The CTA is an open door, not a closing line.

## 10. Guardrails Checklist

**The website must NEVER feel:** corporate · sales-driven · over-designed · artificial · trendy ·
flashy · overly masculine · intimidating · minimal-for-minimalism's-sake · fake-European ·
try-hard-luxury. *Luxury emerges from confidence, not decoration.*

**Run every section through the brief's self-review** — if any answer is "no," keep refining:
- Does it feel like CEO Rules? Would the owner feel understood?
- Does it create emotion before information?
- Does the house (founders) feel central, not siloed?
- Does this section *deserve* to exist? Could it be removed without weakening the arc? (If yes, remove it.)
- Does it feel **timeless**, not trendy?
- Would OnShore Labs proudly showcase this as one of its best projects?

**The final test, above all others:**

> The owner looks at it and says — *"This feels like us."*
